Cardiovascular Biomaterials

Artificial blood vessels do not match the mechanical and biological properties of native tissue, and therefore cannot be used for many important surgeries. The goal of this project was to design an improved artificial artery, using proteins similar to the natural extracellular matrix of cardiovascular tissue.

We began by building a device to produce collagen micro-fiber.1 This device (fig. 1) could yield hundreds of meters of micro-fiber, with an aligned, nanoscopic structure similar to natural tendon (fig. 2).

Fig. 1 Collagen fiber spinning device
Fig. 2 Nanostructure of collagen fiber

Then, we combined this fiber with a genetically engineered version of the protein elastin (fig. 3). Elastin contributes stretch and elastic recoil to natural tissue. By changing the amount and angle of the collegen fiber within the elastin protein, we could dial in the strength and compliance of the artificial blood vessel to match natural vessel.2

Fig. 3 Collagen-elastin artery prototype. (A) Side view, and (B) cross-section. Scale bar: 2mm.

In many tissues, collagen fiber has a wavy micro-structure that makes the tissue more flexible. We developed a micro-fabrication technique to create this micro-structure (fig. 4). The resulting materials matched some of the mechanical properties of natural heart valve leaflet. This part of the project was published in Advanced Materials and highlighted in Nature Materials.3
